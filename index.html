Nursing Philosophy

"Continuity of care is the key to early intervention and emergency prevention."

Nursing, in my opinion, is professional care that uses evidence, clinical judgment, and compassion to uphold human dignity and to assist people in healing, adapting, or living well with their illness. Being a bilingual student of nursing, I have witnessed that the results are significantly better when patients understand the plan and feel comfortable to ask questions. Nurses often know patients best because they spend sustained time with them during daily shifts. Much of nursing involves continuous observation, analysis, and timely intervention when a patient’s condition changes. The National Academies report notes that nurses frequently serve as the first point of access to care, especially in community settings, which makes early assessment and timely escalation a core responsibility (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2021). I want to be the nurse who detects changes in the patient's condition early, who communicates effectively, and who doesn't hesitate to raise the alarm when necessary. My core beliefs are respect, equity, integrity, and service. Respect is about being attentive and tailoring the care to a patient's culture, language, and readiness. Equity involves recognizing the barriers that can make it difficult for people to get care, such as the cost, fear, stigma, or low health literacy, and it is about assisting patients in finding solutions that are not only feasible but also sustainable after discharge. Integrity is about recognizing the boundaries of my practice, adhering to the guidelines, and being truthful in my communication with patients and colleagues. Service is being reliable and continually making the patients benefit the main consideration rather than the easiest way of doing things. I believe that education is the key to autonomy, therefore I focus on instructing and relationship-building as an inherent part of care. As a rights-based profession, nursing also includes culturally safe, people-centered care and advocacy for equitable access to health and health care (White et al., 2025).
